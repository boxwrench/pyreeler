# Creative Lenses

Use this file to decide what kind of piece you are making before choosing techniques.

## Genre and mode

Infer the likely genre, mode, or artistic lineage of the piece. Use that to decide what counts as appealing.

Examples:

- Cosmic or awe-driven work: scale, patience, recurrence, delayed reveal, vastness
- Horror or threat-driven work: interruption, corruption, asymmetry, denied payoff, hostile return
- Ritual or mythic work: recurrence, ceremony, symmetry, invocation, escalation through repetition
- Dreamlike work: unstable continuity, emotional coherence over literal logic, soft transformation
- Techno-futurist work: structural escalation, signal language, hard punctuation, machine logic
- Lyrical or poetic work: cadence, image resonance, restraint, suggestive transitions

## Motif

- Decide the core motif of the piece.
- Let motifs return when they deepen meaning.
- When a motif returns, change something: scale, density, timing, palette, framing, texture, or distortion.
- Avoid accidental recurrence caused by habit or convenience.

## Repetition and rupture

- Repetition is not automatically bad.
- Repetition is useful when it establishes, ritualizes, escalates, or transforms.
- Rupture is useful when the piece needs a break in its established logic.
- Surprise can come from silence, delay, restraint, inversion, mutation, or overwhelming escalation.

## Time

Treat time as material.

- Pace is not just duration. It is spacing, delay, silence, recurrence, and when things are withheld.
- Rhythm is not just editing speed. It is the felt cadence of motion, sound, and return.
- A strong piece often has one moment where time changes character: pause, collapse, acceleration, suspension, or impact.
- A moment of deliberate quiet or stillness immediately before the peak resets the viewer's threshold and amplifies the impact without requiring the peak to escalate further.

## Judgment

Ask:

- What would the obvious version of this piece look like?
- What would make it more specific and less generic?
- Where should the piece satisfy expectation?
- Where should it break expectation?
- What is the one move the viewer is likely to remember?
